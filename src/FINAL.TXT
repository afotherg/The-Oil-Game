DEFSTR business_1911-1 {[newpage][twocol]Business and financial page[hdline]Accounting: a science without rules?[title]Accounting is a difficult discipline. It is usually possible to massage figures into almost any desired shape and as a result there has to be an extensive and intricate system of conventions as to how financial statements should be compiled.[para]The aim here is merely to demonstrate some common ways of accounting for transactions.[onecol][para]Take an example: a company buys a piece of land for $1 million. What happens? Well, this is a redistribution of assets and doesn't affect the income sheet at all (the latter relates to [bbold] operational [ebold] costs). What happens is that in the balance sheet [bitalic] cash in hand [eitalic] decreases by the $1m that has been spent, and [bitalic] land [eitalic] is increased by the same $1m. Therefore the net value of the company's assets [buline]  remains the same, [euline]as does the company's reserves. Now suppose at some later date this piece of land is resold for $2m. Cash evidently increase by $2m, and 'land' is reduced by the original purchase price ($1m). Therefore the value of 'assets' goes up by $1m, and there must be a corresponding increase in liabilities: this is shown by an addition of $1m to reserves. In fact, by selling the land at a profit of $1m the company has increased its value by the same $1m, and this is shown up on the balance sheet (remember 'reserves' measures company value). The profit ($1m) is also usually shown as income on the income statement.[flush][endstr]}

DEFSTR business_1912-1 {[newpage][twocol]Business and financial page[onecol][hdline]Double entry[title]Some more thoughts on double entry accounting.[para]In the last issue we looked briefly at the thorny issue of double entries and the eternal mystery of debits and credits. This would give a pre tax profit of $1000 - $600 - $100 = $300. Suppose the tax rate was 50%, then the post tax profit would be $150. How is all this shown up in the balance sheet?
